THE STORY

The Flying Fez and the Sound of History: My Days as Hasan Basri

Fall 2022 (11th Grade) School & District Auditorium
Arts & Leadership

It was an autumn evening in 11th grade. While the modern world bustled outside, our stage was transformed into the cold, hopeful Ankara of the 1920s. We were preparing a play in memory of Mehmet Akif Ersoy, and I was cast as Hasan Basri Çantay—the MP for Balıkesir and Akif's spiritual brother. If Akif was the storm, Hasan Basri was the anchor.

A Rehearsal to Remember: "The Flying Fez"

Rehearsals weren't always solemn; sometimes, the comedy of errors took over. I remember one particularly intense practice session. I was delivering a passionate monologue about the fate of the nation. I got so caught up in the emotion of the character that I jerked my head emphatically, and my red Fez (the traditional Ottoman hat) went flying off my head, rolling comically across the stage to settle at "Akif's" feet.

The tension in the room shattered instantly. We all—cast and teachers alike—erupted into laughter. Our director joked, "Son, your ideas are so powerful even your hat couldn't handle them!" That moment of shared laughter bonded us more than any perfect rehearsal could have. It reminded us that beneath the costumes, we were just teenagers trying to carry the weight of giants.

The Magic of the Spotlight

But on opening night, the atmosphere shifted. When the curtain rose and the spotlight hit me, the audience disappeared. I wasn't a student anymore. Looking into my friend's eyes, I didn't see a classmate; I saw the weary, burning eyes of a poet fighting for his country's soul.

There was a specific moment during the scene where the National Anthem is being written by candlelight. I placed my hand on Akif's shoulder to comfort him. The silence in the auditorium was absolute—heavy, profound, and electric. We weren't acting; we were channeling history. When the curtain finally fell, I was trembling—not from nerves, but from the sheer emotional intensity of having lived, for an hour, in 1921.
