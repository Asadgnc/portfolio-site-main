THE STORY

The Height of the Minbar: Guiding Thousands at Sahra-i Kebir Mosque

May 2024 (12th Grade) Sahra-i Kebir Mosque, Ümraniye
Leadership & Community

Towards the end of 12th grade, as my high school journey was coming to a close, I volunteered for a task that was both a tremendous honor and a serious test of character. I took on the responsibility of acting as the Imam for the Friday (Jumu'ah) prayer at Sahra-i Kebir Mosque, one of the largest and most magnificent mosques in Ümraniye. This wasn't just about leading a ritual prayer; it meant ascending the minbar (pulpit), delivering the sermon, and shouldering the spiritual weight of the thousands of people standing behind me.

I was not alone on this spiritual journey. My closest friend, Cemil Arslan, stood by me as the Muezzin. While I stood at the altar wearing the Imam's robe, he supported me from the muezzin's gallery. For two young friends to coordinate such a massive congregation was a moment of shared pride and solidarity.

Looking Through the Imam's Eyes

When I climbed to the top step of the minbar and turned to face the congregation, I saw a limitless sea of people. Looking through the eyes of an Imam is a completely unique sensation. From the youngest child to the oldest man, from the wealthy to the working class, everyone stood shoulder to shoulder in silence, waiting for my words.

Normally, facing such a massive crowd would be terrifying. But instead of fear, I felt a focused, positive energy. This wasn't my first time on a "stage." Years of playing lead roles in school theaters, participating in poetry recitals, and speaking at major events like the TÜGVA dinners had prepared me for this exact moment. My past experiences had built a muscle memory for public speaking; I knew how to control my voice, how to manage my adrenaline, and how to hold the room.

The Power of Spiritual Unity

As I delivered the Khutbah (sermon), my voice resonated under the vast dome. I intuitively understood the nuances of rhetoric: where to pause for effect, where to raise my voice, and how to keep the congregation's attention locked. The most powerful moment came during the prayer itself. When I pronounced "Allahu Akbar," hearing thousands of people move as one body behind me, the rustling of their clothes, and the unified sound of prostration was an experience that shook me to my core. It wasn't just leadership; it was spiritual unity.

The positive feedback I received afterwards was so impactful that my mentors still invite me to lead prayers when I have time. Being entrusted with such a sacred platform at a young age remains one of my proudest achievements.
