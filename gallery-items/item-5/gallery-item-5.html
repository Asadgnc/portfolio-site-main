THE STORY

The Spirit of Gallipoli: A Leader's Encounter with History

Spring 2023 (11th Grade) Çanakkale City Center & Gallipoli Historical Peninsula
Leadership & History

There was an unwritten rule at our school: the annual pilgrimage to Çanakkale (Gallipoli) was a rite of passage reserved exclusively for graduating seniors (12th graders). I was only a junior. However, as the Student Council President, I had worked shoulder-to-shoulder with the administration on the massive logistics of this event—organizing lists, securing parental permissions, and managing bus schedules. Recognizing my effort, the principal made an exception, saying, "You earned this." For me, joining this trip wasn't just a perk; it was a badge of trust.

First Lesson in the Strait

Crossing the strait by ferry, the first thing I felt was the sheer strategic weight of the geography. This narrow waterway, where Asia and Europe nearly touch, was the "jugular vein" of a nation. Looking across to the Kilitbahir coast, I saw the colossal inscription carved into the hillside: "Stop, Wayfarer! The soil you tread upon unaware is where an era ended." This sentence signaled that this would not be a mere sightseeing tour, but an education in consciousness.

One of our first stops was the Kabatepe Simulation Center. Here, history isn't just heard; it's experienced. Through 3D technology and sound effects recreating the most violent moments of the campaign, I could almost hear the roar of cannons and the cries of soldiers. As a young leader, I found myself wondering: What kind of iron will was required to make the right decisions amidst such dust, smoke, and chaos? I tried to grasp the psychology of Mustafa Kemal and the other commanders in those critical moments.

The Distance Between Trenches: 8 Meters

The place that struck me most profoundly was Bombasırtı (The Ridge of Bombs). When our guide explained that the distance between the enemy trenches and the Turkish trenches here was only 8 meters (meaning certain death), a heavy silence fell. As Atatürk described: "Those in the front trench fall, none surviving; those in the second trench take their place." Yet, there was no fear, no hesitation. Seeing the place where soldiers marched to their deaths—the literate reading the Quran, the illiterate reciting prayers—reminded me how trivial my own daily struggles were.

Walking into the cemetery of the 57th Infantry Regiment, I was moved less by sadness and more by the sheer discipline and sacrifice. This was a regiment where everyone, from the commander to the last private, fell in battle, yet never let their flag touch the ground. I witnessed the pinnacle of "team spirit" and "dedication." Being the heir to a generation that prioritized the lives of those behind them over their own comfort significantly increased the weight of responsibility on my shoulders.

Conkbayırı and the Miracle of the Watch

When we climbed to the summit, Conkbayırı, the wind was blowing fiercely. This was the knot where the fate of the war was tied and untied. I stood at the exact spot where a piece of shrapnel hit Mustafa Kemal Atatürk's chest, only to be stopped by the pocket watch he carried, saving his life. If not for that watch, perhaps the history of the Turkish Republic would have been written very differently. I realized that history sometimes hangs on a millimeter of coincidence, and sometimes on a will of steel.

One of the most remarkable aspects of the trip was visiting Anzac Cove and the memorials for the foreign soldiers. I believe there is no other nation that shows such respect for the dead of the enemy they fought. Reading Atatürk's famous letter to the Anzac mothers—"They are now our children as well"—I understood that true strength lies not just in fighting, but in peace and forgiveness.

The Martyrs' Memorial and Conclusion

We concluded the pilgrimage at the majestic Martyrs' Memorial, rising 41.7 meters into the sky. Looking up at the mosaic of the Turkish flag on its ceiling, I felt that its foundation was mixed not with mortar, but with the souls of 253,000 people.

Leaving Çanakkale, I looked out the bus window one last time. This trip added volumes to my intellectual knowledge, but more importantly, it gave substance to the word "responsibility." I knew now that I no longer had the luxury to say "I can't" in the face of difficulties, because 15-year-olds in those trenches never said "I can't."
