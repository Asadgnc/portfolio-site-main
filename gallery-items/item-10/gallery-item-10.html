THE STORY

Obsessed with Perfection: Behind the Scenes of 12th Grade

January 2024 (12th Grade) High School
Achievements

The first semester of 12th grade wasn't just an academic year; it was a testing ground for my nerves. I held the title of "Student Council President," but for me, it wasn't a cool badge of honor—it was a handcuff that stripped away my luxury to fail. You can't stumble when everyone is watching. The thought of someone whispering, "He's the president, but his grades are mediocre," was unbearable. It became a matter of honor.

Enemies at the Table

The real battle wasn't against the exams. It was against the five people closest to me. From the outside, we were best friends; but the moment test scores were released, an invisible wall went up. This wasn't some fairytale "friendly competition"; it was a nerve-wracking race where we tracked each other by the millimeter.

If one of them scored even a single point higher than me, I couldn't sleep that night. It wasn't jealousy; it was an intolerance for being left behind. The force that kept me studying until dawn wasn't idealism—it was the fear of losing.

Interrogating the Teachers

During that period, I dropped the "polite student" act. To my teachers, I was probably the student they wanted to avoid in the hallways. The recess bell was their escape signal, but for me, it was the moment to catch them. I wasn't just asking questions; I was extracting answers. "Why is this like this? Why doesn't the rule apply here?"

My incessant questioning wasn't curiosity; it was an obsession. Understanding 99% of a topic wasn't enough; that 1% of ambiguity ate me alive. I could see the "Him again?" look in their eyes, but I didn't care. I wouldn't leave that room without the answer.

97: The Victory of Stubbornness

After all the chaos, sleeplessness, and frayed nerves, I took a deep breath when report card day arrived in January. Average: **97.**

I was at the top of the school.

This score wasn't a product of my intelligence; it was a product of my obsessive stubbornness. Playing the "leader" role at school while emerging first from that wolf-pack rivalry was the ultimate answer I gave to myself. That day, I realized that winning isn't just about working hard—it's about being obsessed.
