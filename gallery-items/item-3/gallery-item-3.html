THE STORY

Following Yunus Emre: From Student Council President to National Delegate

May 11-13, 2022 Kızılcahamam, Ankara
Leadership

During my sophomore year (10th grade), I shouldered one of the most significant responsibilities of my life. As the Student Council President and official representative of my school, I was invited to the "Young Volunteers on the Path of Yunus Emre" summit in Ankara. This was not just another school trip; it was a mission to justify the trust of my entire school community and step onto a much larger stage.

The Setting & Hospitality: Honored Among the Pines

We were welcomed by Kızılcahamam, the scenic retreat of Ankara known for its thermal springs and nature. Nestled among mountains and dense pine forests, the facility was breathtaking. Instead of standard dormitories, we were accommodated in luxurious, villa-style houses that offered great comfort.

The attention to detail by the organizers was commendable: the culinary experience, featuring a rich variety of delicious meals served from morning till night, made us feel not just like students, but like valued guests of the state. This level of hospitality truly made us feel special.

Scale of the Summit & Memorable Encounters

The scale of the event was massive, bringing together nearly 3,000 active students, teachers, and youth leaders from all corners of Turkey. The most memorable aspect was the presence of prominent political figures and party leaders. They didn't just deliver speeches from a podium; they sat with us, engaged in genuine conversations, and listened to our ideas.

Having the opportunity to stand face-to-face with policymakers and ask them questions was incredibly empowering. The schedule was packed with competitions, intellectual games, and passionate discussions.

A Formative Experience

This two-day summit served as a veritable "university of leadership" for me. I connected with peers who shared similar goals, analyzed different student governance models, and discussed real-world strategies for solving educational challenges.

I returned from Kızılcahamam carrying not just fond memories and the taste of great food, but a deepened commitment to public service and a clear vision of what the future of youth representation should look like.
