THE STORY

A Journey to History on Wheels: A Sunny Çanakkale Diary

Spring 2024 (12th Grade) Istanbul - Çanakkale
Events & History

Amidst the intense exam marathon of 12th grade, my classmates and I broke free from the school walls and set off toward a completely different atmosphere. In the early hours of the morning, before the sun had fully risen, we piled onto the buses. We had a journey of about 3.5 to 4 hours ahead of us, but no one cared; with music playing, backseat conversations flowing, and the scenery passing by, we didn't even notice how time flew.

The Favor of the Weather and Our Luck

The most critical factor determining the fate of this trip was undoubtedly the weather. Çanakkale is famous for its wind and rain; if it had rained, walking through the muddy trenches would have turned into torture, ruining the entire trip. But luckily for us, the sky was crystal clear. The sun wasn't just shining above us; it was bathing the sorrowful yet proud geography of Gallipoli in golden light. That fresh air and sunshine felt like a gift given to us to shake off the stress of the entire year.

The Ferry, Seagulls, and Kilitbahir

One of the most enjoyable moments of the trip was when the bus boarded the ferry. As we crossed to the opposite shore on the cool waters of the strait, tossing simit (bagels) to the seagulls trailing behind us, we were greeted by one of history's most aesthetic structures: Kilitbahir Fortress. Built by Fatih Sultan Mehmet during the siege of Istanbul to secure the strait, we were in awe of the architectural genius when we learned it was designed in the shape of a "three-leaf clover" or a "heart" when viewed from above. Right next to it, the famous inscription "Dur Yolcu" (Stop Wayfarer) silently greeted us.

Where Engineering Ends: Seyit Onbaşı

As we toured the peninsula by bus, our first serious stop was the Mecidiye Bastion. Here, we stood at the very spot where the legendary event that changed the course of the war took place: The point where Corporal Seyit (Seyit Onbaşı) shouldered a massive 276-kilogram shell after the cannon's crane mechanism failed. Our guide explained that this wasn't just physical strength, but an explosion of faith and adrenaline. That shell hitting the battleship "Ocean" and burying it in the waters of the strait was not just a military victory, but the story of restarting from a point where everyone said, "it's over."

8 Meters Between Trenches: Bombasırtı

One of the places that made me think the most was Bombasırtı. It is the close-contact point mentioned in Atatürk's memoirs, unparalleled in the world. The distance between the enemy trench and our trench was only 8 meters. Meaning certain death. It was the place where those in the back, seeing those in front becoming martyrs, leaped forward without a moment's hesitation—those who knew how reciting the Quran, those who didn't reciting the Shahada. When you step on that soil, you realize that what happened there was not just a war, but a massive test of will.

The Finale: The Martyrs' Memorial

The peak of the trip was the Çanakkale Martyrs' Memorial at the very tip of the peninsula. When you arrive beneath this massive monument, rising 41.7 meters high, you feel tiny. The Turkish flag mosaic on its ceiling and the reliefs around it told the intensity of the war through the language of art. Walking there among the symbolic tombstones, we saw thousands of nameless heroes lying side by side, having come from all corners of the country, and even from the old lands of the empire. We hadn't just toured, we had learned; we hadn't just seen, we had felt.
